Essay IV

The Engineering of Exhaustion

And Hypernormalisation

Leo Cunningham

There is a peculiar fatigue amongst the people.

Not the honest fatigue that follows a hard day in the hills but something else. This is a tiredness with static in it. A low-grade electrical hum in the blood. It feels like an overuse injury of the mind.

You see it everywhere. In the face of someone staring at their phone in the queue, thumb twitching like an evolving insect. In the person who has not sat in silence for years and no longer knows what their own thoughts sound like before they have been edited by AI. In the executives with eyes like polished shoes, expensive but empty, wired to the teeth and quietly unravelling behind good tailoring and immaculate taste in whisky.

We have built a world that talks endlessly about convenience while quietly strip-mining the human nervous system.

Exhaustion is no longer an accident, it's an industry that did not begin with the smartphone.

It began when we realised that human beings are not rational creatures who occasionally feel, but instead we are feeling creatures who occasionally rationalise.

Edward Bernays understood this in the 1920s. The nephew of Freud, he grasped that if you could attach a product to an identity, you did not need to argue its merits. You needed only to attach it to belonging, fear, liberation, status, or shame. His "Torches of Freedom" campaign did not sell cigarettes. It sold emancipation.2

In 1947 he wrote openly about it in The Engineering of Consent.1 The phrase is almost too honest.

The early public relations men needed newspapers and parades. Today the machine sits in your pocket and learns you faster than you learn yourself.

That is the thing most people have not fully admitted yet. They still speak as though they have merely "fallen behind" or "lost balance" or "need a holiday." They still believe their tiredness is a personal error, a private failure of discipline, a weakness to be corrected with better habits, a supplement stack, a retreat, a mindfulness app, a new planner, a cleaner morning routine. They still think the machine is neutral.

It isn't.

A great many of the systems surrounding us now are built on a simple commercial truth. A rested, centred, self-possessed human being is hard to manipulate. Hard to sell to. Hard to frighten. Hard to herd. Hard to bait into outrage. Hard to keep scrolling. Hard to trap in the endless loop of deficiency and purchase.

A tired person is easier. Shoshana Zuboff calls this surveillance capitalism.3

At first the story sounds almost harmless. Google refined its search engine by analysing user behaviour. Then it discovered that behavioural surplus, the leftover data trails of human action, could predict future behaviour. That prediction could be sold.

From there the escalation was inevitable.

The Facebook emotional contagion experiment in 2014 showed that news feeds could be subtly adjusted to alter mood at scale.4 No one knocked on doors. No one issued commands. The architecture simply shifted what was seen.

Alexa entered the kitchen. Smartphones began tracking location, habits, micro-pauses in scrolling. Target predicted pregnancies from purchasing data before families had announced them.8

They know when you hesitate, when you linger, when you are tired or lonely. And a tired organism is predictable and predictability is profitable. A fragmented person is easier. A person who doubts the world or himself by breakfast is perfect for business.

So we have engineered an environment that frays attention, disturbs sleep, amplifies comparison, rewards performative productivity, and keeps the organism in a mild but continuous state of alarm. Then we sell relief back to the casualties in monthly subscriptions.

The genius of it is not merely that it drains you. It convinces you that the draining is your fault.

You did not optimise hard enough, journal enough, build your brand, track your recovery or wake at 5 a.m. with sufficient hunger.

It really is a magnificent piece of architecture. There is no spectre here, no smoke-filled room and no one has to force you into chains when instead they can simply train you to fasten them yourself and call it ambition.

And this then is the architecture of modern life. Every platform seeps into your attention for its own purpose. Every news item is tuned for urgency, not understanding. Every algorithm learns what unsettles you and serves a little more of it. Biases, heuristics, behavioural switches. A work of art. Even the kitchen table becomes an annex of the office and your nervous system never receives the signal that you are separate from the system.

Cognitive Exploitation

The uncomfortable truth is this: none of this works unless it works on something real.

And what it works on is us. The human brain evolved for speed, not philosophical purity. It evolved to survive long enough to reproduce, not to decode media systems or navigate twenty-four-hour news cycles. It consumes roughly twenty percent of the body's energy. That is an expensive organ. So it economises. It creates shortcuts.

These are biases and heuristics. Think of them as rules of thumb that allow rapid decisions under uncertainty.

In the savannah, for example, of course this was elegant. If something rustled in the grass, assume threat first and we'll figure it out later. The cost of a false positive was possibly embarrassment, but the cost of a false negative was probably death.

In our modern digital ecosystem, those same shortcuts are exploited at scale.

Let's take the negativity bias. We are neurologically primed to attend more strongly to negative stimuli than positive ones. "Bad is stronger than good," as Baumeister and colleagues summarised in their 2001 review of psychological literature.5 Negative headlines travel faster. Fear retains attention longer. Outrage binds groups more tightly than calm agreement.

Now imagine a revenue model built on engagement. Which content would you optimise for?

The confirmation bias compounds it. We instinctively seek and remember information that reinforces our pre-existing beliefs. It reduces cognitive load and protects our manufactured identity. It makes us feel quite coherent. An algorithm, therefore, doesn't need to persuade you to adopt a view, instead it merely needs to learn which views keep you on the platform and feed you more of the same.

Then there is the availability heuristic. We estimate the frequency of events based on how easily examples come to mind. If you are exposed to dramatic crime stories daily, you will feel the world is more dangerous than it statistically is. If you are served constant political outrage, you will feel collapse is imminent.

None of these are moral failings but they are features of human cognition and features become vulnerabilities when understood asymmetrically.

Bernays understood this long before machine learning. But today, the process is fully automated.

Platforms run real-time behavioural experiments. A/B testing headlines. Measuring pause duration, tracking scroll depth and mapping emotional responses. The 2014 Facebook "emotional contagion" study demonstrated that altering the emotional tone of a news feed could measurably influence user mood.4

Not with speeches, but with sliders.

When behavioural science meets algorithmic optimisation, persuasion ceases to be rhetorical and becomes architectural.

You are not being argued with, you are being nudged. Repeatedly, subtly and relentlessly.

And the cumulative effect is not ideological conversion, it is depletion.

Cognitive exploitation does not need you to believe a lie. It only needs you slightly destabilised. Long enough to keep consuming.

Our language has been hacked and weaponised and here is something else happening beneath the exhaustion. Adam Curtis calls it hypernormalisation.6

The term came originally from late Soviet Russia, a condition in which everyone knew the system was absurd, knew the narrative was hollow, yet continued to perform belief because there was no visible alternative.

We know the news cycle is tuned for outrage and we know algorithms amplify division. We also know advertising manipulates fear and scarcity and of course we know corporate greenwashing exists. Governments run digital influence operations, in fact there are 81 countries identified in the Oxford Internet Institute's 2020 report.7

We know this, and yet we scroll. We adapt.

Hypernormalisation is not ignorance, it is exhausted compliance. It does not float in the abstract. It is reinforced daily by a designed narrative architecture.

Media Framing and Greenwashing

Consider something simple. A headline.

During the Iraq conflict, different media outlets described the same military engagement using entirely different language. Some wrote "War in Iraq." Others wrote "War on Iraq."9 A single preposition quietly assigns agency. Location or aggression. Containment or invasion. The facts may remain technically intact, but the moral framing shifts under your feet. This is not theatrical manipulation, it is structural selection.

Editors choose which verbs carry weight. Which images lead, which statistics are foregrounded and which are buried beneath paragraph seven. The modern newsroom operates under the same commercial pressure as every other platform: engagement and engagement favours an emotional charge.

Research in media psychology has shown that negative framing increases attention retention and sharing.5 Threat travels faster than balance. Conflict spreads further than context and the outrage spike outperforms the calm explanation. So what do you think happens when you place that inside an advertising-funded ecosystem?

Major media institutions have accepted sponsorship and native advertising from fossil fuel corporations while simultaneously reporting on climate change. Energy companies sponsor climate summits. Oil giants fund sustainability podcasts. "Net zero" messaging appears adjacent to investigative reporting. The boundary between journalism and public relations becomes somewhat porous. This is not a conspiracy. It is an incentive structure.

When advertising revenue underwrites information infrastructure, certain forms of friction are quietly discouraged. They're not always censored but let's say deprioritised.

Add to this the documented presence of state-sponsored digital influence campaigns. As I have said before, the Oxford Internet Institute identified organised social media manipulation in 81 countries.7 Not all of them are designed to persuade. In fact, it is fair to say that many are designed to agitate, to polarise and to exhaust.

Shake the jar long enough and the insects will fight. The effect on the individual is subtle but cumulative. Events no longer become encountered events but pre-packaged narrative with a voltage to get you jacked-up.

War is dramatised, climate is politicised and economic instability is personalised with you left carrying the emotional baggage of a global theatre. All of this is magnificently done whilst you knock back the first coffee in the morning with TV on.

Hypernormalisation, then, is not merely a psychological adaptation but reinforced daily by an information economy that has created its own vortex of anti-stillness. Right now, stillness is precisely what your nervous system needs to remain stable.

Unfortunately we do not rest, we "recover." We do not think anymore, instead we "process." We don't speak, we curate and we do not live anymore, instead we manage bandwidth.

It sounds sensible at first and some of it is useful. But taken together, it forms a climate. A climate in which the human being begins to experience himself less as an organism, a real human, and more as a node on a system under perpetual maintenance. Everything is transactional.

A system under perpetual maintenance is never at peace.

We should be wary of technology. Not in a luddite sense, but in the sense Roger Crisp described in 1987: the subversion of autonomy. What we must do is reclaim ourselves and harness technology.

We are taught, subtly and repeatedly, that our worth is conditional. We must be useful, visible, improving, agreeable, successful, resilient, informed, attractive, and emotionally literate on demand. We must signal the right values, hold the right views, display the right concern, and react at the right speed. We must be driven but not difficult to manage. We are supposed to be strong but soft, available and always productive.

This architecture is cultural, social and moral and it began much earlier than the inbox.

The Body and Physiology

All of this might be tolerable if it were purely intellectual but of course it isn't.

The organism does not experience headlines just as headlines. It experiences them as signals of threat, instability, comparison or urgency. Your nervous system doesn't differentiate cleanly between some rustle in the bushes and a flashing red banner that says Breaking News.

An arousal takes place and the sympathetic nervous system becomes activated. Cortisol rises, your attention narrows, the heart beats faster and micro-tensions build across your jaw, diaphragm and pelvic floor. This system, of run, fight, freeze and then discharge was designed for something much deeper within the human body — survival.

But we do not discharge, do we? Instead we scroll into a form of medicated oblivion.

We experience hundreds of low-grade threat cues every day. You know the ones — political volatility, war, division, economic uncertainty, social comparison, performance metrics, digital notifications. It's a kind of relentless cumulative ambient outrage.

Now, the body interprets repetition as reality and then chronic activation without completion becomes our new baseline. Our sleep becomes fragmented not because we are weak, but because the organism never receives the full signal of safety. The inbox and the feed and the pings are permanently on station and a device sits by the bed like the loaded stimulus that it is.

The 3am wake up is not a mystery, it is simply unresolved arousal and over time the system adapts to this elevated tone. What once felt like stress now feels, well, normal. The funny thing is, it almost seems that we have come to praise what is, biologically speaking, sustained dysregulation.

It is important to remember that we are built for closeness to people and our natural environment. Our nervous systems settle through simple things like a heartbeat, a glance, a spring day and a steady pace. Our ancestors lived in communities that were small enough to know everyone's face or name and the pace was slow enough to heal conflicts before the end of the day. Now, that circle has exploded beyond control. Instead of settling things with the faces of our local community, we're carrying the chaos of the entire world.

Our nervous system carries wars we cannot influence, scandals we cannot verify, arguments we did not start, expectations we did not choose. And because the brain evolved to bias threat over tranquillity, it gives those inputs priority. Our organism becomes ever vigilant without a resolution.

Chronic stress alters the immune system, it impairs digestion, blunts our libido, tightens the breath, narrows our brain and reshapes our perception. It can even reduce prefrontal flexibility, which is the very cognitive capacity required for critical thinking.

Exhaustion is not just tiredness. It is a debt, accrued by asking a nervous system designed for exertion and recovery to operate on continuous alert. Need a holiday? Of course you do. But from what?

The ridiculous twist to all of this is that our culture offers stimulation as a remedy. Coffee for fatigue, content for boredom, productivity systems for overwhelm and wave after wave of dopamine hits for emptiness. And so the loop tightens and the real self becomes a residual ache in the bones.

The body keeps the real score. It does not care what your LinkedIn says or how you've time-blocked your calendar for the next 6 years. It knows only load, recovery, threat, safety, rhythm, breath, movement, sleep, food, touch, meaning. When these are distorted for long enough, it speaks in a shallow breath in your chest.

You sleep when you should be awake and wake when you should be asleep. You can no longer feel the joy of simple things that were once the most important. You have become so far removed from your true self you have forgotten where your role, the performer ends and the real you begins, if it even still exists.

So what do we do? Drink and drugs? Media? Socialising? Possessions? We push harder.

Then comes the second industry, waiting just beyond the first with all the never-ending sticking plasters.

This is where modern exhaustion becomes truly profitable. Now you can be monetised for the symptoms. Courses, gurus, coaches, pills, retreats, breathwork bundles, high-performance methodologies, trauma-informed productivity systems, and a thousand polished promises that all whisper the same thing.

You can remain in this world exactly as it is. Just buy this and you'll be better. Some of them even work for a while. Not all help is false. Some of it is excellent. Some of it saves lives. I am not mocking genuine therapy, good medicine, wise teachers, or useful tools. I am talking about the broader pattern. The way an entire culture now feeds off the wound while rarely addressing the theft.

A person may spend ten years "working on oneself" while never once asking whether the environment they return to each day is fundamentally the problem. So we are becoming highly trained in the adaptation to conditions without ever confronting the root cause.

That is the dark elegance of the thing. Exhaustion is the symptom. The architecture is deeper. Call it what you like. The effect is the same: it weakens your resistance, narrows your imagination and makes people less likely to question the premises of the absurd life they are trapped in.

And yet, beneath all of this, I believe that some people know. They don't know why and they can't put a finger on it but they demand answers. And they will systemically search and demand in vain for answers and proof — "show me your proof" they say. But this is anthropology ringing a bell deep inside your being.

I have witnessed a man who has been speaking in abstractions for years become suddenly clear after carrying a pack up a mountain in the howling wind and rain. The static drops, words come back and he remembers what matters.

This is not nostalgia. It is physiology. It is common sense.

Human beings require friction, but the right kind. We need effort, but effort in contact with reality. We need challenges, yes, but a challenge that produces coherence and not fragmentation. We certainly don't need infinite digital access that dissolves our essence. We need light and dark, we need sun and rain, we need to feel weather on our skin and consequence in our hands.

Most of all, we need time to be ourselves. Not time in which someone is constantly trying to reach us or to sell to us, or scare us into division, war, consumption and sticking plasters.

The world will keep trying to harvest your attention and call it opportunity. It will keep offering stimulation in place of meaning and management in place of mastery. It will keep manufacturing new forms of fatigue and selling them to you as progress.

Let it.

Not all doors lead to the same place. Some are different. In fact, they look so ordinary that you could easily mistake them as meaningless. They are without the engineered gloss of seduction. But on the other side is something the culture cannot package: a human being no longer available for engineered exhaustion.
